How the High-3 works
The 2,756 divisor explained

Your hourly rate is your annual GS salary (including locality) divided by 2,756 — a 53-hour week × 52 weeks — not the usual 2,087 used for non-firefighters. This produces a lower hourly rate. That is not a pay cut; it is a different way of expressing the same annual salary across a longer standard workweek.

Under 5 CFR 550.1305, creditable basic pay is every hour of your regular scheduled tour at that straight-time rate — including hours that technically qualify as "overtime" because they exceed 40/week. Only these are excluded: the half-time premium on FLSA overtime, irregular overtime and call-backs, and Sunday/night/holiday differential pay.

Result: Because the hourly rate is derived from a 53-hr week but you are credited for all 56–72 scheduled hours at straight time, your creditable FF Base Pay runs a few percent above the published GS salary.
Why the highest 3 consecutive years matter

High-3 is the average of your three highest consecutive years of creditable basic pay. A shift from a 56-hour to a 72-hour schedule during that window meaningfully raises your High-3 — and therefore your lifetime pension. A promotion to a higher GS grade also raises it. Which years fall in that window is worth planning for.

Note that overtime pay itself does not raise your High-3 — only your scheduled tour (56 vs. 72 hours) and your GS grade/step affect FF Base Pay. Use the optional rows above if you want to compare a window that includes an upcoming schedule or grade change.

TSP essentials
FERS matching — capture the full agency contribution

FERS employees get: 1% automatic agency contribution (regardless of what you put in), plus dollar-for-dollar match on your first 3% of FF Base Pay, plus 50 cents on the dollar for the next 2%. Full match requires you contribute at least 5% — the agency puts in 5% and you put in 5%, for 10% total into your TSP from the first dollar of your career.

The match is computed against FF Base Pay, not gross pay — so the higher FF Base Pay from this calculator is the right figure to use.

The five core funds, plus the L Funds

TSP has five individual funds, each with a different risk/return profile:

G FundGovernment securities. No risk of loss of principal; returns track short-term Treasury rates (~4–5%). The only fund guaranteed not to lose value, but barely outpaces inflation long-term.
F FundBond index (U.S. investment-grade bonds). Moderate, steady returns; moves opposite to interest rates.
C FundS&P 500 index — large U.S. companies. ~10%/yr long-run average, but with significant year-to-year swings.
S FundSmall/mid-cap U.S. stocks (everything outside the S&P 500). Higher growth potential, higher volatility than C.
I FundInternational developed-market stocks. Adds geographic diversification; returns vary with currency and foreign markets.

The L Funds (Lifecycle) are pre-mixed combinations of all five, automatically rebalanced and gradually shifted toward G/F as the target date approaches. L 2035 or L 2040 are reasonable defaults if you plan to retire around that year — the fund handles the rebalancing automatically. L Income is the most conservative option, designed for retirees already drawing down their balance.

Most planners suggest moving toward income-oriented allocations (L Income, or a heavier G/F mix) in the 5–10 years before retirement to reduce the chance of a market downturn hitting right before you need the money.

Roth TSP vs Traditional TSP

Traditional TSP: contributions reduce taxable income now; withdrawals — both contributions and growth — are taxed as ordinary income in retirement. Better if you expect a lower tax rate in retirement.

Roth TSP: contributions are after-tax; qualified withdrawals (both contributions and growth) are tax-free. Better if you expect a higher rate later, or want tax-free income in retirement to reduce AGI and thus your Medicare premiums (IRMAA).

The 59½ catch: a Roth withdrawal is tax-free on the growth portion only if it is "qualified" — meaning both (a) 5 years have passed since your first Roth TSP contribution, and (b) you are age 59½ or older (or disabled). If you withdraw before 59½, the growth portion of a Roth withdrawal is taxed as ordinary income — even though your original contributions come out tax-free either way. TSP withdrawals are pro-rata, so you cannot choose to withdraw "just contributions" the way you sometimes can with a Roth IRA. See the next section for how this interacts with retiring at 50.

Given the substantial other income (annuity + SRS + household income) you may have in early retirement, Roth contributions during high-earning working years can still be valuable — but run the numbers with a tax professional.

Withdrawing TSP early — the special-provisions (age 50) exception

Normally, TSP withdrawals before age 59½ trigger a 10% early withdrawal penalty on top of regular income tax. However, federal firefighters, law enforcement officers, and air traffic controllers have a specific exception: if you separate from federal service in or after the year you turn 50 (which covers the 25-years-at-any-age and 50-with-20-years retirement paths), withdrawals from TSP are exempt from the 10% penalty — even though you are well under 59½.

What this exception does not change: it removes the 10% penalty only. It does not make a Roth withdrawal "qualified." So:
  • Traditional TSP: withdraw penalty-free starting at retirement (age 50+); pay ordinary income tax as usual.
  • Roth TSP: withdraw penalty-free, but if you are under 59½ and have not met the 5-year rule, the growth portion of each withdrawal is still taxed as ordinary income (pro-rata with your contributions). Once you turn 59½ and the 5-year clock has run, Roth withdrawals become fully tax-free.

Practical takeaway: a firefighter retiring at 50 can tap traditional TSP penalty-free immediately. For Roth TSP, the contribution portion is always tax- and penalty-free, but the growth portion carries an income-tax bill until 59½ and the 5-year holding period are both satisfied — plan withdrawal sequencing accordingly, and confirm your situation with a tax professional before separating.

SS claiming age comparison
Key insight: Claiming at 62 means 5 years of earlier payments but a permanently reduced benefit. Waiting to 67 or 70 means a larger check for life. The breakeven is typically around age 78–82.
How the 62/67/70 figures are calculated: SS takes your highest 35 years of inflation-adjusted earnings, averages them (your "AIME"), and runs that average through a formula (your "PIA") to get your age-67 benefit. Age 62 = PIA reduced for claiming early; age 70 = PIA increased for delaying. If you stop working before age 62 with fewer than 35 years of earnings, the missing years count as $0 in the average — pulling all three figures down. ssa.gov's projection assumes you keep earning until each claiming age, so it can overstate your real benefit if you retire early. To estimate your own: log into ssa.gov, look at your year-by-year earnings record, and mentally zero-out any years between your retirement and age 62/67/70 — the effect is roughly proportional across all three ages, so the 62/67/70 relationship stays similar even if all three numbers shift down a bit.

SRS rules in plain language
How the SRS amount is calculated

Formula: SRS = (age-62 SS estimate) × (FERS years ÷ 40)

Example: $1,800/mo SS estimate × (23 ÷ 40) = $1,035/mo SRS. Because your federal years are typically less than 40, the SRS is a fraction of your full SS benefit.

The age-62 figure from ssa.gov overstates your actual benefit as an early retiree because OPM assumes continued earnings. The real benefit will be somewhat lower. The SRS itself is not affected — it uses the ssa.gov figure as the base.

The earnings test — when it applies and what it costs

For special-category employees (firefighters, LEOs, ATCs) who retire before their MRA (age 57), the earnings test does not apply to the SRS until the year you reach your MRA. After that, wages or self-employment income above the annual exempt amount (~$23K, indexed) reduce the SRS by about $1 for every $2 over the limit.

Your spouse's income does not count. Only your own earned income (wages, self-employment net) counts.

Practical implication: If you retire at 50 and stay home until 57, no earnings test applies during those years. If you take a part-time job earning $35K, the test starts at age 57 and would reduce the SRS by roughly $6K/yr — but you were also earning $35K, so the net picture is still positive.
Coordinating SRS with actual Social Security

The SRS stops the month you turn 62 — you then have to decide when to claim actual Social Security (62, FRA ~67, or 70). These are two completely separate decisions:

  • The SRS amount is fixed by the formula and cannot be increased by waiting.
  • Actual SS grows ~8%/year for each year you delay from 62 to 70.
  • You can take the SRS in full and still wait to claim SS at 70 — the two do not interact.

If you are in good health and have other income during ages 62 through 70, waiting to claim SS at 70 is often optimal, as it maximizes the inflation-protected lifetime annuity.

FERS special provisions — eligibility & formula
Eligibility (5 U.S.C. 8412(d))

A covered 0081 firefighter can retire with an immediate, unreduced annuity at:

  • Age 50 with 20 years of covered special-provisions service, OR
  • Any age with 25 years of covered service.

There is no age-based reduction for retiring before 55 or 62. Mandatory retirement is age 57 with 20 years. Covered employees pay an extra 0.5% of basic pay into FERS each pay period. Unlike regular FERS, COLAs apply to the annuity before age 62.

Annuity computation (5 U.S.C. 8415(d))
1.7% × High-3 × (first 20 years)  +  1.0% × High-3 × (years beyond 20)

Example: $95,000 High-3, 25 years → (34% + 5%) × $95,000 = $37,050/yr base annuity before survivor reduction.

Unused sick leave counts toward the computation (adds service years) but NOT toward eligibility for the special-provisions retirement.

Survivor election & FEHB continuity

Electing a survivor annuity reduces the basic annuity: a 50% survivor benefit reduces your annuity by 10%; a 25% survivor benefit reduces it by 5%.

Continued FEHB eligibility for a surviving spouse generally requires at least some survivor annuity to be in place. Treat the survivor election and health-coverage continuity as a single decision — you cannot un-elect survivor benefits after retirement without specific circumstances.

Pay mechanics — the High-3 foundation
The firefighter pay framework (5 U.S.C. 5545b)

The 1998 firefighter pay reform created a special pay structure. Under 5 CFR Part 550, Subpart M:

  • Hourly rate = annual GS rate (with locality) ÷ 2,756 (53 hrs/wk × 52)
  • Creditable basic pay = straight-time rate × all scheduled tour hours, per pay period × 26
  • The differential question: under Subpart M, a firefighter generally may NOT receive separate Sunday/night/holiday premium pay (5 CFR 550.1306). If your 25% differential is paid as a distinct premium, it does NOT count toward your High-3. If it is paid under the older 5 U.S.C. 5545(c) standby regime (up to 25%, capped at GS-10), it IS creditable. Check your LES/SF-50 pay code.

  • Read your LES / pay authority to identify whether your differential is 5545(c) standby (creditable) or a separate premium (not creditable).

  • Contact TSP at tsp.gov for your current balance, fund allocation, and beneficiary designation.
